Benchmarking MILP Reformulations of the MNL Assortment Problem

Comparing three linear/MIP reformulations of the mixed multinomial-logit (MNL) assortment problem on computational tractability, implemented in GAMSPy. RWTH Marketing Analytics module (M.Sc. Data Analytics & Decision Science), supervised by Prof. Sven Müller. Ongoing.

The problem

Assortment optimization asks which subset of products to put on the shelf to maximize expected revenue when customers choose according to a mixed multinomial-logit (random-parameters logit) model. The problem is NP-hard, and the expected-revenue objective is nonlinear: each segment's choice probability puts the offered product's attraction weight in the numerator and the sum over the whole offered set in the denominator. That reciprocal structure has to be linearized before the problem can be handed to a MILP solver, and how you linearize it changes how hard the model is to solve.

Three reformulations, one problem

  • Méndez-Díaz et al. (2014, Discrete Applied Mathematics): introduce a reciprocal variable, linearize the resulting bilinear term with McCormick inequalities, add valid inequalities, and solve by branch-and-cut.
  • Haase & Müller (2014, EJOR): a probability-space / constant-substitution reformulation that avoids the bilinear term entirely (originally single-segment, so a segment weight is added to compare fairly).
  • Şen, Atamtürk & Kaminsky (2018, Operations Research): the same MILP backbone as Méndez-Díaz, plus a conic reformulation and tighter, capacity-aware (conditional McCormick) bounds.

What I've built so far

  • Unified notation (done): rewrote all three formulations — each from a different source with its own symbols and assumptions — into one consistent notation, delivered as a compiled LaTeX report.
  • Linearization (done): derived the McCormick / big-M linearization of the bilinear reciprocal term that the Méndez-Díaz and Şen backbones share, and worked through how the valid inequalities and the conic / conditional-McCormick bounds tighten the LP relaxation.
  • In progress: reproducing MNL choice probabilities on a small worked example, generating artificial instances to Şen's numerical-study design, and implementing all three models in GAMSPy to produce a like-for-like tractability comparison (root gap, end gap, solve time, branch-and-bound nodes).

Why it matters: the three formulations describe the same optimal assortment, but a solver chews through them very differently — the whole point is to measure which is cheapest to solve and how that cost scales with the number of products. The same model family underpins recent work on when the simple revenue-ordered assortment heuristic is reliable, i.e. when a fast shortcut suffices versus when the exact MILP is worth its cost.
